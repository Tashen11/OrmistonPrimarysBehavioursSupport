Ormiston Primary School Strategies for Behavior Intervention

SELECT A BEHAVIOUR

Anxiety

  • Worry about everyday things and are unable to control them.
  • Frequently ask for help when trying to do tasks that they can do independently.
  • Apprehensive to start and work independently. Need constant reassurance once they get started.
  • Seem unsure of themselves and have low self-confidence and self-esteem.
  • Do not smile often. Unable to relax.
  • Easily startled. Have a hard time concentrating.
  • Assure the learner that everyone gets stressed/frustrated/worried/ overwhelmed.
  • Validate what the learner is experiencing: “I know you are frustrated because you feel this assignment is too hard”.
  • Normalize the feeling: “I also get frustrated when I think something is hard and so do other learners”.
  • Teach practical relaxation/regulation techniques - deep breathing, count to 10, write in a journal.
  • Scaffold tasks- break tasks into smaller parts, allow breaks after each part is completed, and allow them to do the easy or hard items first, whichever they prefer.
  • Teach coping skills- recognize body signs (sweaty, racing heart, etc), label the emotion, use strategies that help you regulate (have a drink, go to a place to calm down, attempt an activity of interest (drawing, fidget toys, etc).
  • Allow proximal seating. Check on them often.
  • Reflective listening- listen to what learner says and repeat it back to them so they know you heard and understood them.
  • Seek advice from pastoral care.
  • Discuss with SLT.
  • Hui with parents.
  • Ask parents to seek a GP appointment if they have concerns regarding the anxiety too.
  • External referral to health or social services.
  • Seek Counselling support- private sessions through the parents or 3 free sessions through the GP.
  • Provide 1:1 mentoring.
  • Check-in, check-out. This consists of learners checking in daily with an adult at the start of school to retrieve a goal sheet and encouragement, LCs provide feedback on the sheet throughout the day, learners check out.
  • Use non-verbal cues and signals to indicate if feeling overwhelmed.
  • Provide sensory tools and toys.

Defiance

  • Lash out verbally at others. Engage others in arguments and conflict.
  • Unwilling to let issues go or drop them.
  • Dislike being told what to do. Do the opposite of what is told.
  • Smile, cross arms, stomp feet, etc when asked to follow directives. Strong verbal protest.
  • Become aggressive and act out when told to do something.Refuse to comply with adult requests. Refuse to follow classroom routines.
  • Only enjoy tasks or activities they like or enjoy. Tantrums and meltdowns when asked to do something else.
  • Acknowledging positive behavior- behaving appropriately, paying attention, being on task, prepared for work, sitting quietly, and being kind to others.
  • Avoid power struggles. Do not match the emotional level of the learner, remain calm, state the expectation, and walk away.
  • Clear and concise directions. Use a calm neutral tone.
  • Provide clear and acceptable choices. Have the learner decide in 10 seconds or you choose for them.
  • Seek advice from pastoral care.
  • Discuss with SLT or redirect learner to SLT.
  • Hui with parents.
  • Time out of habitat. Redirect to office.
  • Time out of iBreak.
  • Goal setting, monitoring, and daily tracking sheet.

Aggression/Bullying

  • Verbally or physically harass others.
  • Engage in bullying activity, intimidation, threats.
  • Be observed hitting, kicking, and repeatedly pushing others.
  • Demonstrate intense anger.
  • Frequently lose temper or have blow-ups.
  • Hurt or interact roughly with others during play, recess, or free time.
  • Name call.
  • Instigate and be involved in frequent conflicts, arguments, and fights.
  • Don’t make a huge deal out of it.
  • Remain in control of the environment. Redirect to established rules and expectations of the habitat.
  • Minimize verbal instructions. Use non-verbal signals to redirect/ calm down. Use choices.
  • Redirect to Zones of Regulation and tool-kit for strategies.
  • Clearly define and consistently enforce rules regarding respectful behavior, bullying, and aggression. Ensure the learner understands the consequences of such behavior.
  • Intervene Early. It can prevent behavior from escalating and sends a message that such actions are unacceptable.
  • Use Restorative Practices like group conversations, taking responsibility, and reflecting on one’s actions.
  • Encourage open communication. Create a safe space for students to express their concerns and feelings.
  • Seek advice from pastoral care.
  • Discuss with SLT or redirect learner to SLT.
  • Hui with parents.
  • Time out of habitat.
  • Time out of iBreaks.
  • Slow release back into habitat or playground.
  • Restorative conversation.
  • Daily behavior tracking.

Disrespectful/Confrontational

  • Frequent engagement of confrontation with adults and peers.
  • Frequent talking back to adults.
  • Destructive of others’ property.
  • Take and use others’ things without asking or caring about personal space.
  • Dismissive of others.
  • Unwilling to consider others’ ideas and opinions.
  • Have no regard for others’ feelings, well-being, and safety.
  • Lash out verbally at others.
  • Challenge the authority of the adult.
  • Refuse to comply with adult requests.
  • Refuse to follow habitat routines.
  • Acknowledge positive behavior. When the learner is seen behaving appropriately, paying attention, being on task, prepared for work, sitting quietly, being kind to others, etc., make a remark about how happy or proud you are.
  • Avoid power struggles. LC to remain calm.
  • Redirect. Ask the learner to find a space to calm down. Give a break.
  • Explain the assignment/directions to the learner using eye contact. Have the learner repeat the directions to you.
  • Provide consequences matching the severity of the behavior, e.g., missing ibreak, privileges withdrawn.
  • Walk away. Don’t engage in a conversation about it.
  • Ask learner to reflect on behavior using the Reflection sheet.
  • Seek advice from pastoral care.
  • Discuss with SLT or redirect learner to SLT.
  • Hui with parents.
  • Time out of habitat.Redirect to office.

Off-task/Disruptive

  • Annoying and distracting to others.
  • Pestering.
  • Ask a lot of obvious questions.
  • Make frequent and unnecessary comments and questions.
  • Doing everything other than what they should be doing.
  • Talk to others frequently.
  • Throw objects.
  • Yell out.
  • Avoid power struggle. DO NOT match the emotional level of the student, rather remain calm, cool, and collected.
  • Explain assignment/directions.
  • Simplify tasks.
  • Explain the assignment/directions to the learner using eye contact and getting down to their level.
  • Have the learner repeat and explain each part of the directions back to you.
  • Move to a new location. Team up with a buddy.
  • Praise when on task. Make praises specific, personalized, and individualized. Eg. “I like how you have brought your folder/work and are all ready for your learning today”.
  • Seek advice from pastoral care.
  • Discuss with SLT or redirect learner to SLT.
  • Hui with parents.
  • Restorative conversation.
  • Time out of habitat to complete task.
  • Time out of iBreak to complete task.

Upset/Crying

  • Have frequent emotional meltdowns.
  • Cry frequently over “small” things like lost place in line or wrong answer.
  • Appear distraught, disturbed, or sad.
  • Have trouble pulling themselves together and calming down.
  • Refuse to be comforted, push others away.
  • Be unable to talk or verbally express concerns.
  • Rock, hold head or face in hands, turn away or back to others, put hand up, shake head “no.”
  • Not knowing what they want when asked.
  • Ask to be left alone, run away, or sit in a corner in the habitat.
  • Put hand to mouth and flutter hands and/or arms.
  • Make statements like “I can’t do this,” “This isn’t happening,” “I can’t take this,” etc.
  • Provide reassurance and encourage deep breathing.
  • Speak in a calm, neutral voice. DO NOT match the emotional state of the student. Stay neutral in your demeanor and ask only factual questions.
  • Redirect to strategies from the Zones of Regulation toolkit.
  • Move to a breakout room or another space in the habitat.
  • Instill confidence in the learner: “I know you can do it and I think you know you can too.”
  • Allow time to calm down. Give choices when ready.
  • Teach coping skills at a different time so they are more ready for a difficult situation next time.
  • Reflective listening: listen to what the learner says and repeat it back to them so they know you heard and understood them.
  • Seek advice from pastoral care.
  • Discuss with SLT (Senior Leadership Team) or redirect the learner to SLT.
  • Hui (meeting) with parents to understand triggers.

Not Ready to Learn

  • Appears confused and distracted.
  • Lacks focus on the task.
  • Shows a lack of engagement with the task.
  • Quietly blends in while doing nothing, doodling, or pretending to work.
  • Spends excessive time searching through things (desk, locker, etc.).
  • Say they are getting to the task or are working on something and produce few results.
  • Does not have necessary tools for the lesson (e.g., books, writing tools).
  • Lacks confidence or has difficulty with comprehension and memory skills.
  • Difficulty following verbal instructions.
  • Difficulty following complex instructions.
  • Has poor organizational skills.
  • Gives many excuses.
  • Assign a buddy to assist with transitions/setup/organization.
  • Break down assignments/ simplify instructions. Have them do the easy or the hard items first, whichever they prefer.
  • Allow the learner to check back to you after the completion of a small section of the task.
  • Give 1:1 support at the beginning, make eye contact, and gain attention.
  • Redirect to the Zones of Regulation toolkit. Redirect to strategies that can get them ready to learn.
  • Use a visual planner/ schedule for all activities and tasks. This can include information on what group the learner is in, what is required before each session etc.
  • Explain the assignment/directions to the learner using eye contact. Have the learner repeat the directions to you.
  • Seek advice from pastoral care.
  • Discuss with SLT or redirect learner to SLT.
  • Hui with parents.
  • Time out of the habitat to complete the task.
  • Time out of iBreak to complete tasks.

Lack of Social Skills

  • Interacts and navigates their environment in an awkward or odd manner.
  • Antagonizes others.
  • Annoy, poke, hit, tap, or get the attention of others then run away.
  • Have poor concept of appropriate times and places to say certain things.
  • Teases others frequently.
  • Has younger or more immature friends.
  • Seems emotionally immature.
  • Has difficulty making or maintaining friendships.
  • Displays inappropriate behaviors.
  • Always try to top others, be better, win every time.
  • Poor winner and loser.
  • Plays alone or parallel play.
  • Acts silly in an attempt to make others laugh.
  • Is overprotective of relationships.
  • Assign a buddy.
  • Teach appropriate ways to interact with others/ opportunities for social interaction.
  • Teach social skills.
  • Move to a new location.
  • Focus attention without calling attention in a negative manner.
  • Seek advice from pastoral care.
  • Discuss with SLT or redirect learner to SLT.

Tantrums/Out of Control

  • Fails to respond to redirection, calming, or de-escalation efforts.
  • Extremely oppositional and defiant.
  • Deliberately destroys items, furniture, toys, supplies, etc.
  • Yells, screams, hits, kicks, bites, flails, stomps, rolls on the ground, crawls, hits head on things.
  • Lays on the floor and refuses to do what is asked.
  • Runs out of or around the room.
  • Runs out of school.
  • Expresses no concern for consequences.
  • Runs from adults and authority figures.
  • Yells “no”, “I don’t want to”, “I don’t have to”, “Make me”, etc.
  • Avoid power struggles. Keep responses brief, to the point. Avoid lecturing, talking at length, or patronizing.
  • Use a calm and neutral tone. DO NOT match the emotional level of the student.
  • Try stating the expectation and consequence, tell the student the choice is theirs, and walk away.
  • If the student looks too distressed, walk away or stand at a distance, looking away.
  • Offer several choices, give a timeline to decide within, and walk away.
  • Try re-direction if learner is able to be de-escalated.
  • Remove student from situation and discuss the issue after time to calm down.
  • Use reflective listening (e.g., “I hear that you’re feeling upset”).
  • Count to 10, redirect to Zones of Regulation strategies if the learner seems receptive.
  • Move to a breakout room or a quieter corner in the habitat.
  • Seek advice from pastoral care.
  • Discuss with SLT or redirect learner to SLT.
  • Hui with parents.
  • Refer to RTLB if the behavior is ongoing and Tier 1 strategies have not been effective.
  • Do a well-being check to rule out other reasons for the emotional outbursts.
  • Create a behavior de-escalation plan in consultation with SLT.
